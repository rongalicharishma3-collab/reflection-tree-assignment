Reflection Tree Design Write-up
1. Introduction

The reflection tree is designed to guide users through a structured process of self-reflection. The goal of this system is to help individuals analyze their behaviors, reactions, and motivations in professional situations. The tree is structured across three psychological axes: self-awareness, contribution to others, and growth mindset. These axes represent a progression from understanding oneself to understanding one’s impact on others and finally focusing on long-term development.

2. Question Selection

The questions in the tree were selected to represent common workplace situations and personal decision-making patterns.

The first axis (Self-Awareness) focuses on how individuals react to challenges and how often they reflect on their actions. Questions such as reactions to difficult situations and daily reflection habits help identify whether a person tends to avoid responsibility, blame external factors, or analyze problems constructively.

The second axis (Contribution) focuses on teamwork and collaboration. Questions explore how individuals respond when teammates need help and how they define success. These questions help identify whether someone prioritizes personal achievements or collective team outcomes.

The third axis (Growth Mindset) focuses on learning and improvement. Questions examine how individuals respond to failure and what motivates their long-term development. These questions aim to encourage reflection on learning from experiences rather than focusing only on results.

Each question includes 3–5 fixed options, ensuring consistent branching and predictable paths through the tree.

3. Branching Strategy

The reflection tree uses a combination of question nodes, decision nodes, reflection nodes, and bridge nodes.

Question nodes gather user responses and determine the next branch in the tree. Decision nodes interpret the meaning of the responses and guide the user toward reflective insights. Reflection nodes provide short interpretations that help users reconsider their mindset or behavior.

Bridge nodes are used to transition between the three axes of the reflection process. The first bridge transitions from self-awareness to contribution, while the second bridge transitions from contribution to growth mindset. This design ensures a logical progression through the reflection journey.

The branching strategy balances clarity and depth. The tree contains enough nodes to explore multiple behavioral patterns while still keeping the structure readable and easy to trace.

4. Psychological Foundations

The design of the reflection tree was inspired by several psychological and behavioral theories:

Growth Mindset Theory (Carol Dweck) – emphasizes learning from failure and continuous improvement.
Self-Reflection and Metacognition – the ability to analyze one’s own thinking and decision-making processes.
Locus of Control Theory – distinguishes between individuals who attribute outcomes to internal actions versus external factors.

These theories support the idea that structured reflection can encourage individuals to move from reactive behaviors toward more proactive and growth-oriented thinking.

5. Possible Improvements

If more time were available, the reflection system could be expanded in several ways. Additional nodes could be added to explore deeper behavioral patterns and provide more personalized feedback. The system could also include a scoring mechanism to track signals across each axis and generate more detailed summaries.

Another improvement would be to develop a visual dashboard that displays reflection insights and patterns over time. This could help users track personal growth and identify recurring behaviors.

6. Conclusion

The reflection tree provides a structured approach to personal and professional reflection. By guiding users through self-awareness, contribution, and growth mindset, the system encourages individuals to think more critically about their actions and motivations. The design emphasizes clarity, logical progression, and psychological grounding, making it a useful tool for structured reflection and personal development.
